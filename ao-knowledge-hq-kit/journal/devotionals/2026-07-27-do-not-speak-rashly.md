## Scripture

**Proverbs 29:20 (ESV)**

## Reflection

Proverbs places hasty speech below foolishness on its scale of concern. That is a severe assessment. A fool at least has the excuse of lacking wisdom. A person who speaks hastily has the capacity to think before they speak and simply chooses not to exercise it.

Haste in speech is a choice, usually an unconscious one. Leaders speak quickly because the pace of the environment rewards fast responses. Because silence feels like indecision. Because they have always moved quickly and no one has named the cost. But haste is a choice, and Proverbs names the consequence of that choice more bluntly than almost any other passage on communication.

The leader who speaks hastily will say things they cannot take back. They will commit to things they cannot deliver. They will wound people they intended to help, confuse people they intended to direct, and damage trust they intended to build. And they will do all of this repeatedly, because haste is a pattern, not an isolated incident.

The remedy is not slower speech for its own sake. It is the development of a pause as a non-negotiable practice between stimulus and response. Not a long pause. Not a conspicuous one. Just enough space for the leader to evaluate whether what is ready to come out is what should come out.

That pause is where wisdom enters the conversation. Without it, whatever is present internally simply transfers directly to the room.

## Practical Application

- Identify where haste in your speech has cost you more than slowness ever would have.
- Build a consistent pause before responding in the settings where haste is most tempting.
- Choose one conversation this week where you deliberately slow down and notice what it produces.

## Takeaways

- Hasty speech is a choice, usually unconscious, and Proverbs treats it more severely than foolishness.
- A pause between stimulus and response is where wisdom enters the conversation. Without it, whatever is internal transfers directly to the room.

## Closing Thought

The leader who pauses before speaking will say fewer things they regret and more things that matter.
