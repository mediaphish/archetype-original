## Scripture

**Proverbs 18:13 (ESV)**

## Reflection

Proverbs does not soften this. Answering before hearing is folly. And it produces shame. Not just a missed opportunity or a communication gap. Folly and shame.

Leaders answer before they hear more than they realize. The position creates it. They have been hired to have answers. The room expects them to know. Silence feels like absence of leadership. So leaders speak, often before the full picture has been given, and frequently before the person in front of them has finished communicating what they actually came to say.

The cost compounds. A leader who answers before hearing gives a response shaped by incomplete understanding. They solve the wrong problem. They correct the wrong thing. They offer direction that does not fit the actual situation because they did not wait long enough to understand what the actual situation was.

There is also a relational cost. The person who was not fully heard before the answer came knows it. They register it even when they do not say so. Over time, people stop bringing their full picture to a leader who answers before it can be delivered. They edit themselves in advance, knowing the response is coming before the context is complete.

## Practical Application

- In your next conversation, commit to letting the other person finish completely before you respond.
- Notice how often you begin forming your answer before the person has finished speaking.
- Identify one relationship where answering before hearing has cost you accurate understanding.

## Takeaways

- Answering before hearing produces responses shaped by incomplete understanding. You solve the wrong problem.
- People stop bringing their full picture to a leader who answers before it can be delivered.

## Closing Thought

The leader who hears fully before answering will give better answers than the one who speaks first every time.
