## Scripture

**1 Peter 3:9–10 (ESV)**

## Reflection

Peter connects the quality of a leader's speech directly to the quality of their life. Whoever desires to love life and see good days. The life that is worth living, the days that are worth having, are shaped in part by what comes out of the mouth.

The negative version is named first. Repaying evil for evil. Reviling for reviling. These are the reflexive communication patterns that leaders fall into under pressure. Someone speaks poorly to them and they return it. Someone challenges them and they respond with the same energy they received. It feels justified in the moment. Peter names it as the path away from the life and the days worth having.

The positive version requires something more than restraint. It requires blessing. Not silence. Not neutral response. Actively speaking something good into a situation that provoked something bad. That is not a communication skill. It is a character requirement, and it is one that accumulates over a leadership lifetime.

Verse ten grounds the whole thing in a simple standard. Keep the tongue from evil. Keep the lips from deceit. The leader who does this consistently, not perfectly but consistently, is building something in their own life and in the lives of the people they lead that reactive, retaliatory speech could never produce.

## Practical Application

- Identify where you have been repaying communication patterns in kind rather than redirecting them.
- Notice the cost that reactive speech has produced in your relationships and your own internal state.
- Choose the blessing rather than the repayment in one specific situation this week.

## Takeaways

- The quality of a leader's speech is directly connected to the quality of the life they are building.
- Repaying evil for evil feels justified in the moment and costs more than it gains over time.

## Closing Thought

The leader who refuses to repay in kind is not being passive. They are protecting something worth more than the satisfaction of the response.
