# Small Slips, Big Culture Cost

Culture does not usually collapse in a single dramatic hour. It shifts through **permission**: the ambiguous priority that never gets clarified, the standard bent for a favored project, the hard conversation postponed until “after this quarter,” the cynicism laugh-track that teaches everyone what is truly endorsed.

In ALI, **Drift** names whether conditions are holding—or **silently sliding**. It is not a moral trophy category like trust or clarity; it is the pattern where small behavior changes stack into cultural consequences.

---

### What drift looks like before the drama

Drift shows up as rising silence, subtle inconsistency, hesitation around decisions, communication distortions, emotional compression, or gradual avoidance of accountability. Any one item might be a bad week. Together they tell a directional story.

Servant leadership refuses to shame leaders for being human. It **does** insist on honesty: drift is measurable because behavior leaves footprints long before headlines arrive.

---

### Why drift loves tolerance

Organizations drift when shortcuts feel compassionate in the moment—avoiding conflict, softening standards to reduce friction, letting ambiguity persist to keep peace. Compassion without courage becomes decay wearing kind language.

Correcting drift starts with tiny repairs: reclaim clarity in plain words, restore consistent enforcement without favorites, shorten feedback loops, invite candor again.

---

### Directional hope

The encouraging truth is directional: drift can be interrupted. Early correction asks for smaller adjustments than late rescue—because you are rebuilding patterns, not reputations from ashes.

---

Drift is not destiny. It is a warning light—asking leaders to notice the wake before the storm fully forms.

When leadership responds early, cultures don't just survive quarters; they learn that truth-telling and steadiness are how we actually care for people—not only how we talk about caring.
