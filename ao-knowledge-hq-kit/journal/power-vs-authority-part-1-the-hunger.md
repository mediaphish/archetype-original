# Power vs. Authority: Part 1 — The Hunger

There is a particular kind of leader who enters every room already calculating.

Not calculating the problem in front of them. Not calculating what the team needs or what the situation requires or what a good decision would look like. Calculating the position. Who has influence here. Who is watching. What this moment could mean for where they are headed. What it would cost them to be wrong in front of these people.

It happens fast enough that most people who do it have long since stopped noticing they are doing it. It has become reflex. The calculus runs in the background, underneath the surface of normal conversation, underneath the stated agenda, underneath whatever meeting they are technically in. They are present in the room. They are somewhere else entirely.

That reflex is what this series is about.

Not the behavior of the already-powerful, though that behavior matters and we will get to it. This first part is about the hunger that precedes the power. The wanting. The specific quality of ambition that is pointed not at building something but at becoming something. At getting somewhere. At securing a position that cannot be taken away.

That hunger is more common than most organizations know. It is more costly than most leaders admit. And it starts earlier, runs deeper, and does damage longer than the moment anyone would recognize it as a problem.

---

## What We Mean When We Talk About Power

The word is slippery. Power gets used to describe everything from organizational authority to personal presence to the ability to get something done. For the purposes of this series, a distinction matters from the first page.

Power, as we are using it here, is the capacity to compel. It comes from position, from title, from control over resources, from the ability to reward or punish. It is granted from above or seized from circumstance. It exists whether or not the person holding it has done anything to deserve the trust of the people beneath it. It is structural. It operates through hierarchy. It does not require relationship. It requires compliance.

Authority is different. Authority is the capacity to lead people who could, in theory, choose not to follow. It is influence that exists independent of the org chart. It is what a leader retains when the title is removed, when the budget is gone, when the position changes and the people who used to report to them still pick up the phone. Authority is earned. It is built through a specific kind of behavior sustained over time. It cannot be granted. It cannot be taken. It can only be built or not built, maintained or lost.

These two things look identical from the outside at the moment of compliance. A team that follows because they are required to and a team that follows because they trust their leader look, in the moment of following, exactly the same. The difference appears under pressure. Under crisis. Under the conditions where compliance is not enough and what is actually needed is commitment.

Most organizations never examine the difference until they are already paying for it.

## Where the Hunger Comes From

Most people who pursue power do not sit down one day and make a decision about the kind of leader they are going to be. The hunger does not arrive fully formed with a strategy attached. It grows from something that was, at its origin, entirely legitimate.

Roy Baumeister's decades of research on human motivation established that the drive toward status and recognition is not a flaw. It is wiring. Humans are social animals whose survival has always depended on standing within the group. The need to be seen, to be valued, to matter to the people around you is not vanity. It is evolutionary. Early in a career, the ambition to prove something, to build something, to be recognized for something is almost indistinguishable from genuine contribution. The hunger and the work point in the same direction. The results are real. The recognition is earned.

The problem is what happens next.

The recognition arrives. The promotion comes. The title changes. And instead of satisfying the hunger, the arrival feeds it. The person who worked hard to become a manager now wants to become a director. The director wants the VP seat. The VP is watching the C-suite. At each rung, the external validation of the previous level becomes the floor, not the ceiling. The appetite recalibrates upward. The drive that produced the first result is now chasing the next marker, and the nature of what is being chased has quietly changed.

This is the moment Baumeister's research points to as the inflection point. The reward systems that reinforced the drive in the early stages, promotions, titles, compensation, visibility, the approval of people above, are designed to reward the external markers of success, not the internal qualities that created them. And the person receiving those rewards begins, gradually and often without awareness, to optimize for the markers rather than the qualities. The behavior that was originally aligned with contribution becomes aligned with advancement. The shift is not dramatic. It is incremental. It is the accumulation of a thousand small decisions where the question "what does this require?" gets quietly replaced by the question "what does this do for me?"

Most people who make that shift never notice it. They are still working hard. Still producing results. Still telling themselves the story that what they are building matters beyond themselves. But the internal orientation has changed, and the people around them begin to feel the difference before the leader is willing to name it.

## The Internal Mechanics

Dacher Keltner's Power Paradox is one of the most important and least-cited findings in leadership research. What makes it important is not that it shows power can corrupt people. That idea is old and uncontroversial. What makes it important is the mechanism it identifies and how early in the arc that mechanism activates.

Keltner's research at UC Berkeley documented that the behaviors most associated with gaining influence in the first place, empathy, active listening, the ability to make other people feel understood, genuine collaboration, awareness of the room, are measurably eroded not by years of power but by the experience of power itself, often within months of a role change. The brain's mirroring systems, the neural architecture that allows a human being to feel something close to what another person is feeling, show reduced activation in people who have been primed to feel powerful. This is not metaphor. It is physiology. The powerful become, in a literal neurological sense, less capable of the emotional attunement that built their influence.

In laboratory settings, people who were primed to feel powerful were more likely to take food from a communal plate intended for others, more likely to interrupt during conversations, more likely to make unilateral decisions even when collaborative options were structurally available, and significantly less accurate in reading the emotional states of the people around them. In field studies of actual organizations, the pattern held. Leaders operating from positional power showed diminished responsiveness to feedback, reduced awareness of team dynamics, and increased certainty in their own judgment even in domains where that certainty was not warranted.

But here is what most people miss when they read Keltner's work. The hunger for power begins degrading these capacities before the power is ever fully obtained. A person in pursuit of a position, optimizing for perception and advancement, is already activating the psychological patterns associated with having power. The ambition itself triggers the orientation. The person chasing the seat begins behaving like someone protecting a seat they do not yet have. And the team around them begins experiencing the consequences of that orientation before anyone has given the person any formal authority at all.

The pursuit is not a neutral waiting room before the real problem begins. The pursuit is where the problem begins.

## What David McClelland Actually Found

Harvard psychologist David McClelland spent his career studying human motivation, and his framework for power motivation is more nuanced and more useful than most people who cite it suggest.

McClelland did not find that the desire for power is inherently destructive. What he found is that there are two fundamentally different expressions of the drive toward influence, and they produce radically different outcomes.

The first is personalized power motivation. This is the drive to accumulate influence for personal gain, for status, for dominance, for the feeling of control. Leaders high in personalized power motivation pursue positions to have them. The accumulation is the point. They are energized by winning, by being seen as powerful, by the experience of other people complying with their direction. They build organizations, sometimes very large and apparently successful ones, but they build them around themselves. The organization exists to express and amplify their power, not the other way around.

The second is socialized power motivation. This is the drive to build influence in order to deploy it for something larger than the self. Leaders high in socialized power motivation are not disinterested in authority. They want it. But they want it as a tool. The accumulation of influence is instrumental, not terminal. They are energized not by compliance but by the kind of collective performance that is only possible when people are genuinely committed to a shared outcome.

McClelland's research found that leaders high in personalized power motivation consistently produced lower team morale, higher turnover, and cultures built on fear and compliance. The organization worked in the same way a clenched fist works, everything held in place by pressure. But the moment the pressure released, through a leadership transition, a market shift, a crisis that required genuine commitment rather than surface compliance, the thing that looked like culture turned out to be posture. People had been performing safety, not feeling it.

Leaders high in socialized power motivation produced something different. Stronger engagement. Higher loyalty. Teams that continued performing in the leader's absence because the leader had been building something real, not just requiring something visible.

The hunger is not the problem. What the hunger is pointed at is everything.

## The Seduction of Not Having to Explain Yourself

Stanley Milgram's obedience research from the 1960s is almost always cited in one direction. People comply with authority. That is real and worth understanding. But there is a less-examined finding in the broader body of work on authority and power that belongs in this conversation.

People who hold power frequently describe a specific and compelling psychological reward that has nothing to do with status or recognition. They describe the relief of not having to justify themselves.

The powerful person does not have to explain the decision. Does not have to sit in the discomfort of being questioned. Does not have to seek consensus from people who might disagree. Does not have to absorb the cost of uncertainty in front of others. Power, among its other properties, offers the person holding it a particular kind of freedom. The freedom from having to be wrong in a room where being wrong has social cost.

This is one of the more honest and less flattering reasons people pursue power, and it is one that almost no one admits to. The hunger is not always about status or recognition or the desire to dominate. Sometimes it is about exhaustion. About having spent enough time being junior, being questioned, being overruled, being required to justify the obvious, that the prospect of a position where that stops feels like relief.

That motivation is understandable. It is also dangerous. And it produces something very specific in the day-to-day behavior of a leader operating from it.

Watch how this leader runs a meeting. The agenda exists, but it is not really a conversation. It is a sequence of announcements dressed as discussion. Questions from the team get answered quickly, sometimes before they are fully finished being asked. The leader is not being rude. They are being efficient. What they are actually doing is closing the window before anything uncomfortable can climb through it. The meeting ends. Decisions were made. Nobody is entirely sure how.

Watch how this leader handles disagreement. When someone pushes back on a decision, the response is not engagement. It is a restatement of the original position, delivered with slightly more authority the second time. If the pushback continues, the tone shifts. Not to anger necessarily. To a kind of patient finality that communicates, without saying it directly, that the conversation is over. The person who pushed back remembers that. They factor it into every future decision about what to raise and what to keep to themselves.

Watch how this leader processes information. They have surrounded themselves, not always deliberately, with people who have learned what kind of input is welcome. The team has done the math. Honest assessments that contradict the leader's existing view create friction and cost the person delivering them something. Assessments that confirm what the leader already believes get received warmly and sometimes rewarded. Over time, the information flowing upward has been quietly filtered by every person in the chain who learned through experience what happens when they deliver something the leader did not want to hear. The leader believes they have good information. They have comfortable information. Those are not the same thing.

Watch how this leader makes decisions under pressure. When a crisis arrives and the moment genuinely requires input from people who are closer to the problem, the leader does not slow down to gather it. Slowing down to gather input means opening yourself to being told something you did not anticipate. It means sitting in uncertainty in front of people who are watching you for signs of confidence. The leader who has built their identity around not having to justify themselves cannot afford that moment of visible uncertainty. So they decide fast and call it decisiveness. Sometimes they are right. When they are wrong, the people who saw it coming say nothing, because they already know what saying something costs.

This is what the relief of not having to justify yourself actually produces inside an organization. Not a single dramatic failure. A slow, structural narrowing of every channel through which honest information and genuine challenge could reach the person making the decisions. The leader is not uninformed because people are hiding things. They are uninformed because they built, decision by decision and interaction by interaction, an environment where bringing them the truth was never quite worth the cost.

Milgram showed that ordinary people comply with authority under ordinary conditions. What his work points toward, when you follow it into organizational life, is that the compliance is not just a response to the person holding power. It is a rational adaptation to the specific conditions that person has created. People are not complying because they believe the leader is right. They are complying because the environment has made disagreement expensive and agreement easy. The leader built that environment, often without ever intending to, simply by being someone who found relief in not being questioned and let that relief shape how they responded when questioning arrived.

That environment does not feel like a problem to the person at the center of it. It feels like a team that trusts them. It feels like alignment. It feels, from the inside, like exactly the kind of organization they were trying to build.

That gap between what the leader is experiencing and what the organization is actually doing is one of the most reliable warning signs available. And almost no one inside the system is positioned to close it, because closing it requires telling the leader something they built the entire environment to avoid hearing.

## What the Research Says About How It Ends

McClelland's work on personalized power motivation already pointed toward how this arc ends. Keltner documented what power does to perception once someone holds it. Baumeister traced how reward systems pull ambitious people toward external markers instead of the qualities that earned them. Thirty-three years of watching this pattern in real organizations fills in the rest.

Leaders oriented toward personalized power often advance faster early. The behaviors that chase position produce measurable career acceleration in the short and medium term. Strategic relationship management, strong self-presentation, willingness to compete aggressively for visible opportunities. All of it moves the needle on advancement.

They also tend to arrive at lower long-term satisfaction. More isolation in senior roles. More late-career professional crisis. The kind where a person who spent decades climbing reaches the position they were chasing and discovers that having it does not produce what they needed it to produce. The hunger was real. The satisfaction the arrival was supposed to deliver was not.

This is not a morality tale. It is a finding. The pursuit of power, as a primary career orientation, tends to produce the external markers it is chasing and to hollow out the internal experience of the person chasing them. The leader who spent 20 years optimizing for position arrives at the position and finds that the strategies that got them there have made them exactly the kind of person who cannot fully enjoy or effectively use what they built.

The relationships are transactional because they trained themselves to treat relationships transactionally. The team is compliant because they built a culture of compliance. The information they receive is filtered because they created conditions where honest information was not safe to deliver. They are at the top of something that was designed, through thousands of accumulated choices, to reflect back to them only what they wanted to see.

And they cannot see it clearly. Because the same orientation that produced the outcome has reduced their capacity to perceive it accurately.

Keltner called it the Power Paradox. I would call it the loneliest outcome available to an ambitious person. To spend a career building something, to arrive at the destination you set for yourself, and to find that the building process itself produced the conditions that make the destination hollow.

## The Diagnostic Nobody Wants

The pursuit of power is not just a character flaw. It is a diagnostic.

How a person pursues influence tells you, with a high degree of reliability, who they will be when they have it. The leader who manages perception on the way up will manage perception from the top. The leader who treats relationships as instrumental before they have any formal authority will treat them as instrumental after. The leader who cannot be honest about a failure when the stakes are low will not suddenly become honest when the stakes are high. The leader who surrounds themselves with people who reflect their own views back to them while they are climbing will build an organization structured around that same dynamic once they arrive.

The hunger is not hidden. Not to the people watching it. Not to the teams who have worked alongside someone chasing a position and felt the specific quality of attention that kind of person pays. Not to the peers who noticed that every conversation somehow circled back to the same person's agenda. Not to the direct reports who learned, through enough accumulated experience, that bringing a real problem forward was more likely to create a problem for them than to produce a solution.

The hunger is visible to everyone in the room. The only person it tends to be invisible to is the person who has it. And that invisibility is not incidental. It is part of the mechanism. McClelland's research, Keltner's research, Baumeister's research all point to the same finding from different angles. The orientation toward personalized power motivation comes with a systematic reduction in the capacity to perceive its own effects. The person pursuing power becomes less accurate, over time, in reading what their behavior produces in others. The feedback loops that would correct the orientation get bypassed, ignored, or eliminated.

The result is a leader who is genuinely surprised when the floor drops. Who could not have told you, six months before it happened, that anything was wrong. Who would have passed a 360 review designed to surface these issues and walked away with data that confirmed what they already believed about themselves.

That is not a failure of intelligence. Some of the most intelligent people I have encountered in this work have been precisely the kind of leader this series is describing. Intelligence does not protect against this. Neither does education, or experience, or a track record of results. The pattern does not discriminate by credential.

What it discriminates by is orientation. Whether the fundamental direction of a person's ambition is pointed toward something larger than themselves, or whether it is pointed back at themselves. That orientation, established early and reinforced by the reward systems of most organizations, determines almost everything that follows.

## The People Left in the Wake

There is another side to this that the research captures in aggregate but that I want to name specifically, because the aggregate obscures something important.

Behind every leader running this pattern, there are people.

People who came into a role with genuine enthusiasm and had it systematically worn down by an environment where their contribution was valued only to the degree it served someone else's position. People who tried to raise something real and learned, through the response they got, that raising real things was not safe here. People who were talented enough to have options and eventually used those options to leave, not with bitterness necessarily, but with a quiet and complete understanding of what the place actually was.

People who stayed, and who adjusted. Who learned to give the leader what the leader wanted to see. Who became skilled at the performance of engagement without the substance of it. Who did their jobs well enough to stay and not well enough to be noticed, because being noticed in a power-driven culture means becoming useful to the person at the top, and becoming useful to the person at the top means being an instrument, and they had decided, at some point, that they were done being that.

The more important cost is not organizational. It is personal. It is what happens to a person who spends years in an environment where their genuine contribution is not the point. Where they are there to serve a machine that is ultimately pointed at someone else's advancement. Where their development, their growth, their wellbeing, their honest experience of the work is not a factor in the decisions being made above them.

That cost does not show up in a Gallup survey. It shows up later. In the way people talk about their careers. In the things they say they wish they had done differently. In the leaders they themselves become, sometimes replicating the patterns they were formed by, sometimes overcorrecting in ways that create different but related problems. In the quiet damage that institutional exposure to power-driven leadership does to a person's baseline trust in the institutions they work within.

This is the full accounting. Not just the organizational metrics. The human cost, paid by specific people, across years of their lives, in exchange for the privilege of working for someone who was using them to get somewhere.

That cost is real. It deserves to be named directly. And any examination of the pursuit of power that does not name it is incomplete.

## Where This Leaves Us

The pursuit of power is not a simple story about bad people doing bad things. That framing is too easy and too clean and it lets too many people off the hook, including people who would recognize themselves in this series if the mirror were held at a slightly different angle.

Most leaders who run this pattern are not villains. They are people who started with a legitimate hunger and followed the reward systems that were put in front of them. People who were never given an honest accounting of what their orientation was costing the people around them, because the systems they operated within were not designed to deliver that accounting. People who genuinely believed, at every stage, that they were doing good work, because the metrics available to them said so and the people around them had learned that saying otherwise was not safe.

They are also, without exception, leading organizations that are operating below their actual capacity. Organizations where the people who could make the most difference have already made a quiet decision about how much of themselves to invest. Where the information that most needs to reach the top is the information least likely to arrive there intact. Where the culture that looks healthy from the outside is quietly running on compliance rather than commitment, and will continue to do so until the moment something requires more than compliance and the gap becomes impossible to ignore.

Part 2 of this series is about what the alternative actually looks like. Not the theory of it. The specific, unsexy, non-linear, often uncomfortable work of building authority rather than pursuing power. What it requires. What it costs. What it produces in the people around you and in the organization you are responsible for.

The hunger does not go away. But it can be pointed at something worth chasing.
